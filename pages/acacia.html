Acacia | Smart Irrigation

Acacia Irrigation has been one of my main projects at The Quadracci Sustainable Engineering Lab. Initially inspired by SharedSolar, another qSEL project, the focus is on finding a more economically feasible way to provide irrigation to smallholder farmers in the developing world. Initially as part of a USAID Powering Agriculture grant three pilot solar systems, 4.8 to 6.8kW, were installed in Gabar, Senegal. They provide irrigation water to twenty-one half hectare farms. This shared model increased the solar utilization with the design requirements of no storage and as consistent water supply as possible. There is no large battery array or water tower included. Water is distributed directly by means of drip lines or stored at ground level in basins for hand distribution. The motivation behind this design decision is economical and based on current farmer practices.

The second iteration of Acacia is targeted towards commercial sales, away from a utility model. Inexpensively adding cloud-based control to off the shelf pumping hardware for individual farmer systems. It is easier to finance a smaller device and with remote data logging and control some maintenance can be provided from afar. If the market allows there is opportunity to sell monitoring services to the farmer as well. In this case, by serving a larger variety of customers, more farmers can be aided with a better service.

Revision One:

The first revision of the Acacia system was controlled by a Raspberry Pi. I created a "hat" PCB for the Pi to integrate all the sensors and switches. A pyranometer and a temperature sensor are used to approximate shortwave radiation and PV cell temperature. Power available is estimated based on those readings. The board also includes a real-time clock for more accurate logs, an RS-485 to USB converter for reading an array of smart meters, and a transistor array for signaling the relays on the power electronics.

A companion web application allowed for local payments, historical record keeping, and automatic scheduling. This revision taught me a lot about the Linux operating system, about Python, the Modbus communication protocol, and electronic prototyping. The three pilot systems have run for five plus years at this point, maintenance is sustained by a PAYGO payment scheme. With any luck they will run for many more seasons.

Revision Two:

The second revision of the Acacia system is controlled with an Arduino style microcontroller. The intent was to simplify the controller as much as possible. Custom PCBs were created to interface with any off the shelf solar inverter or VFD that exposes an RS-485 communication port and a low voltage DC power supply. Only critical data is stored persistently and fluctuations in power are expected. On different versions the controllers either have an IR input or a cellular modem for communication with the cloud. The IR is for lower cost or out of network installations.

A cloud connected IOT device needs a cloud-based web application. This revision communicates with that server where historical usage and payment data can be stored. This revision taught me more about C++ and minimizing memory usage; program memory but more importantly SRAM. The power controller communication can be simulated to simplify testing. The ability to properly test each component, software or hardware, can make all the difference in a production system.
